# Aerospace MRO Glossary

Terms specific to the Aerospace MRO industry and planning domain, as used in the Partcast README.

---

**AD (Airworthiness Directive)**
A mandatory regulation issued by the FAA (in the US) or EASA (in Europe) requiring operators to perform a specific inspection, repair, or part replacement on affected aircraft within a defined compliance window. ADs are triggered by safety findings — a discovered failure mode, a fleet-wide inspection finding, or a manufacturer-identified risk. Because AD compliance is legally required and non-negotiable, an active AD with an upcoming compliance deadline creates a hard demand trigger for the affected part numbers. Unlike a scheduled maintenance event, an AD can be issued at any time and may set a compliance deadline shorter than the supplier lead time for the required part.

**AOG (Aircraft on Ground)**
The condition in which an aircraft is grounded and unable to fly because a required part is unavailable, a mandatory maintenance action cannot be completed, or an airworthiness issue has not been resolved. AOG is the worst-case outcome of a supply failure in MRO operations. A commercial aircraft grounded for an AOG event costs the operator an estimated $150,000 or more per hour in lost revenue, crew costs, and passenger compensation. The term is also used as an adjective (an "AOG part" is a part being expedited to resolve an active grounding) and as a verb informally ("the aircraft was AOG'd for three days").

**AOG Risk Scoring**
A quantitative assessment of the probability that a specific aircraft will be grounded due to a parts shortage, expressed for each tail in the fleet against the current inventory position and forward demand plan. AOG risk is not a uniform metric — it reflects the interaction between a tail's upcoming maintenance events, the criticality of the parts required, the current stock level, and the supplier lead time for replenishment. Scoring AOG risk proactively, before an event occurs, is the primary forward-looking signal Partcast surfaces for supply planners and VP-level operators.

**Airworthiness Certification / Airworthiness Documentation**
The paperwork chain that establishes that a spare part is legally eligible to be installed on a certified aircraft. In commercial aviation, a part cannot be installed without documentation proving its origin, maintenance history, and conformance to type certificate data. The relevant certification forms (such as FAA Form 8130-3 or EASA Form 1) must accompany the part through the supply chain. A part that is physically identical to a required component but lacks complete airworthiness documentation cannot be used — it is not a valid substitute. This is why MRO inventory is serialized rather than fungible: two identical part numbers are not interchangeable if their paperwork chains differ.

**C-Check**
A type of heavy maintenance check performed on commercial aircraft at multi-year intervals, typically triggered by accumulated flight hours or calendar time, whichever comes first. C-checks are the most labor- and parts-intensive scheduled maintenance events in an aircraft's life cycle — they involve partial disassembly of the airframe, inspection of structural components, and replacement of time-limited parts. A C-check on a narrowbody aircraft may take several weeks and require hundreds of distinct parts. Because the parts list for a C-check is largely known in advance, a tail approaching its C-check threshold creates a structured, forward-visible demand signal that is one of Partcast's primary planning inputs. (Other check types include A-checks, lighter routine checks performed more frequently, and D-checks, the heaviest overhaul category.)

**Consumption History**
The historical record of parts actually used — removed from stock and installed on aircraft — over a prior time period. Traditional demand planning platforms are built primarily around consumption history as the forecasting input: they detect statistical patterns in past consumption and extrapolate them forward. In aerospace MRO, consumption history is an incomplete signal because it does not distinguish between planned removals (a part replaced at its scheduled maintenance interval) and unscheduled removals (a part that failed unexpectedly). A part with low historical consumption may have very high forward demand if the fleet's C-check cycle is approaching. A part with high historical consumption during a major fleet campaign may revert to near-zero demand once the campaign ends. Consumption history is a lagging indicator; maintenance event calendars are leading.

**EASA (European Union Aviation Safety Agency)**
The regulatory authority responsible for civil aviation safety in Europe, equivalent to the FAA in the United States. EASA issues airworthiness directives, certifies aircraft and parts, and sets the regulatory framework for maintenance organizations operating on European-registered aircraft. MRO providers servicing European operators must hold EASA Part 145 approval, which governs the standards for maintenance organization procedures, documentation, and personnel.

**ERP (Enterprise Resource Planning)**
The core enterprise software system that most airlines and MRO providers use to manage inventory, procurement, work orders, and financial records. Dominant ERP platforms in aerospace MRO include SAP S/4HANA and Oracle. ERP systems are the authoritative record of inventory on hand and historical consumption, but they are not designed for forward-looking demand planning — they record what happened, not what is likely to happen. Partcast ingests ERP data as a historical baseline and augments it with forward-looking signals that the ERP does not track.

**FAA (Federal Aviation Administration)**
The US regulatory authority for civil aviation, responsible for issuing airworthiness directives, certifying aircraft and parts, and overseeing maintenance organizations operating on US-registered aircraft. MRO providers in the US must hold FAA Repair Station certification (Part 145 approval) to legally perform maintenance on certificated aircraft.

**Flight Hours**
The accumulated hours an aircraft has been in active flight since manufacture or since a major overhaul baseline was set. Flight hours are one of the primary maintenance interval triggers in commercial aviation: most scheduled maintenance events and part replacement requirements are defined in terms of flight hours, flight cycles (individual takeoff-and-landing sequences), or calendar time — whichever threshold is reached first. A tail number's current flight hours, combined with its average daily utilization rate from the flight schedule, determines when it will hit its next maintenance interval threshold.

**FVA (Forecast Value Add)**
A planning metric that measures whether a human intervention in the forecasting process improved or degraded accuracy relative to the model's baseline. FVA is calculated by comparing the accuracy of the final approved forecast (after all human overrides) against the accuracy of the AI-generated forecast that preceded it, measured against actual outcomes. A positive FVA means the human added value; a negative FVA means the human made the forecast worse. In most traditional MRO planning organizations, FVA is not measured at all — overrides are undocumented and their outcomes are never reviewed. Partcast makes FVA a live operational metric, visible to both planners and S&OP leadership.

**IBP (Integrated Business Planning)**
An evolved form of S&OP that extends the consensus planning process to include financial planning, strategic scenarios, and a longer planning horizon. IBP platforms (SAP IBP, Kinaxis) are designed for large enterprises with mature planning functions. In the aerospace MRO context, these platforms are used primarily for aggregate demand sensing and financial reconciliation — they lack the maintenance event data model required to generate event-driven forecasts at the tail-number and part-number level.

**Lead Time (Supplier Lead Time)**
The time between placing a purchase order with a supplier and receiving the part at the MRO facility, available for installation. In aerospace MRO, supplier lead times for critical parts sourced from OEMs are frequently 12 to 18 months — and sometimes longer for new production parts during supply-constrained periods. Lead time is a hard constraint on the planning horizon: if a part is needed in 90 days and the lead time is 120 days, a procurement decision that has not already been made cannot solve the problem. This is why forward-visible demand signals matter so much in MRO — the planning window has to be long enough to act within supplier lead time.

**Maintenance Event Calendar**
A structured schedule of upcoming planned maintenance events across the operator's fleet, showing which tails are approaching check intervals, when each check is expected to occur, and the parts and labor required for each event. The maintenance event calendar is the primary forward-looking demand signal in MRO planning — it defines not just what will be needed, but when. It is typically maintained in the airline's or MRO provider's maintenance tracking system (such as AMOS, Ramco, or Swiss-AS). Partcast ingests the maintenance event calendar as a continuous signal feed, not as a static annual plan.

**Maintenance Program**
The comprehensive technical document, approved by the regulatory authority, that defines all scheduled maintenance tasks required for a specific aircraft type over its service life. The maintenance program specifies which inspections, replacements, and overhauls must be performed at defined intervals (flight hours, flight cycles, calendar time), and what parts and labor each task requires. It is derived from the aircraft manufacturer's maintenance planning document and may be customized for a specific operator's fleet configuration. The maintenance program is the definitive source of truth for what parts will be needed over the planning horizon.

**MRO (Maintenance, Repair, and Overhaul)**
The industry and discipline responsible for keeping commercial aircraft airworthy. MRO encompasses scheduled maintenance (A-checks, C-checks, D-checks), line maintenance (routine between-flight checks), component repair, and engine overhaul. The MRO market is structured around a contractual relationship between the airline (which owns the aircraft and defines maintenance requirements) and the MRO provider (which performs the work, sources the parts, and is contractually obligated to return the aircraft to service within agreed turnaround times). Major MRO providers include Lufthansa Technik, ST Aerospace, MRO Americas, and airline-affiliated operations such as Delta TechOps and Air France Industries KLM Engineering and Maintenance.

**OEM (Original Equipment Manufacturer)**
The manufacturer of the original aircraft component — typically Boeing, Airbus, or a Tier 1 supplier such as Safran, Honeywell, or UTC Aerospace. In aerospace MRO, many critical parts are single-source from the OEM: there is no approved alternative supplier, no spot market, and no substitute. OEMs typically operate with long and often unpredictable lead times, limited production flexibility, and pricing power that reflects the absence of competition. The single-source OEM structure is one of the defining constraints of MRO demand planning — it eliminates expediting as a reliable fallback when a forecast misses.

**Override Attribution**
The practice of documenting, for each human modification to an AI-generated forecast, the specific rationale for the change and the identity of the person who made it. Override attribution serves two purposes: accountability (the plan reflects documented decisions, not undocumented adjustments) and learning (when actuals arrive, the attribution record allows the system to compare AI accuracy against human accuracy by override reason and planner, building the FVA dataset over time). In most current MRO planning processes, overrides are made in spreadsheets with no documentation, making it impossible to determine retrospectively whether the human or the model was right.

**Repositioning**
The transfer of spare parts inventory from one stocking location to another to cover a demand gap. In MRO operations, repositioning is often used as a short-term response when a part is needed at a specific maintenance base but is on hand at another location. Because parts are serialized and each carries its own airworthiness documentation, repositioning requires not just a physical transfer but a documentation transfer — the part's records must travel with it. Repositioning is generally cheaper than expediting a new order but has implications for the inventory position at the originating location that must be accounted for in the demand plan.

**S&OP (Sales and Operations Planning)**
The recurring cross-functional planning process — typically monthly — through which an organization aligns its demand forecast, supply plan, and financial plan into a single consensus view. In MRO operations, S&OP involves the demand planning team (which owns the forecast), the supply chain team (which owns procurement and inventory), the maintenance operations team (which defines the maintenance schedule), and commercial leadership (which manages the airline customer relationship). The S&OP output is the approved demand plan that governs procurement decisions for the planning horizon. In most MRO organizations today, a significant portion of S&OP meeting time is spent reconciling conflicting data rather than making forward-looking decisions.

**Serial Number**
The unique identifier assigned to an individual part unit, distinct from the part number that identifies the part type. In aerospace, every installed or stockroom component has both a part number (what kind of part it is) and a serial number (which specific unit it is). The serial number is linked to the part's full maintenance history, repair records, and airworthiness documentation. Two parts with the same part number but different serial numbers are legally and operationally distinct. This is the basis of serialized inventory management in MRO — the supply chain must track individual units, not just quantities.

**Serialized Inventory**
An inventory management approach that tracks each individual unit of stock by its serial number, maintenance history, and certification status, rather than treating identical part numbers as interchangeable quantities. Serialized inventory is the standard in aerospace MRO because regulatory requirements and airworthiness documentation requirements mean that parts cannot be substituted without verifying each unit's specific history. This distinguishes MRO inventory management from virtually every other supply chain context, where inventory is typically managed as undifferentiated quantities of a given SKU.

**SLA (Service Level Agreement)**
The contractual document between an airline and its MRO provider defining the terms of the maintenance relationship — including turnaround time commitments (how quickly the MRO provider must return an aircraft to service), parts availability obligations, AOG response requirements, and pricing structures. SLAs directly shape demand planning requirements: a contract that requires a 5-day heavy maintenance turnaround time implicitly requires parts to be on hand or reliably available within that window. SLA terms are a critical input to Partcast's demand planning logic because they define the operational consequences of a supply failure for a specific customer relationship.

**Tail Number**
The registration identifier of an individual aircraft, typically displayed on the aircraft's tail fin (e.g., N12345 for a US-registered aircraft). In MRO planning, the tail number is the primary unit of analysis: maintenance intervals, modification status, AD compliance history, and remaining service life are all tracked at the tail level, not at the fleet level. Two aircraft of the same type and vintage may have meaningfully different maintenance needs if they have different utilization patterns, different modification configurations, or different maintenance history baselines. Partcast generates demand forecasts per part number per tail number — not aggregate statistical forecasts — because tail-level specificity is where the planning information actually lives.

**Unscheduled Removal**
The removal of a part from an aircraft outside of its planned maintenance interval, typically due to an unexpected failure, an inspection finding, or an in-service anomaly. Unscheduled removals are harder to forecast than planned replacements because they reflect failure events rather than known maintenance schedules. They are, however, partially predictable through reliability engineering: parts with high historical unscheduled removal rates on specific fleet types or specific tail configurations can be flagged as elevated-risk in the demand plan. The distinction between scheduled and unscheduled removals is essential to understanding MRO demand — aggregating them into a single consumption signal, as most planning systems do, conflates two very different demand mechanisms.
